# Atomic Structure and Bonding

To understand why different material classes (metals, ceramics, polymers, composites) have vastly different mechanical, thermal, and electrical properties, we must begin at the atomic scale. The type and strength of **atomic bonds** determine stiffness (Young's modulus), melting point, electrical conductivity, and ductility. Lectured by Dr Mark Eaton as part of EN1213/EN1214/EN2101.

---

## Atomic Structure

Every element is made from **atoms** consisting of:
- **Nucleus**: protons (positive charge, determines element type) and neutrons (no charge, contributes to mass)
- **Electrons**: negative charge; orbit the nucleus in discrete **shells**

The number of protons = atomic number (Z). The outer (valence) electrons govern bonding behaviour.

**Electron shells**: fill from the innermost outward. Each shell holds a maximum of 2n² electrons, but the outer shell is satisfied (stable) with 8 electrons (octet rule). The periodic table is structured around this: a new row begins when a new shell starts filling. Elements in the same column (group) have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemistry.

## Types of Atomic Bonding

### Ionic Bonding
Electrons are **transferred** from a metal atom to a non-metal atom, creating charged ions (cation +, anion −). Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions forms the bond.

Examples: NaCl (table salt), MgO, Al₂O₃ (alumina)

Properties:
- High bond strength → high melting points and stiffness
- Directional charge distribution → brittle (ions of the same charge brought adjacent during slip → repulsion and fracture)
- Insulators or semi-conductors (electrons tightly bound to ions)

### Covalent Bonding
Electrons are **shared** between adjacent atoms. Each atom achieves a filled outer shell through sharing.

Examples: diamond (C-C), silicon (Si-Si), polymers (C-C backbone)

Properties:
- Very strong and directional → very high hardness and stiffness (diamond is hardest known)
- Often brittle (directional bonding resists plastic deformation)
- Electrical insulators to semiconductors

### Metallic Bonding
Metal atoms release their valence electrons into a shared **electron cloud** (sea of electrons). Positive metal ion cores are held together by electrostatic attraction to this delocalised electron cloud.

Examples: iron (Fe), copper (Cu), aluminium (Al)

Properties:
- Non-directional → metals are **ductile** (ion cores can slide past each other without breaking bonds)
- Good electrical and thermal conductors (mobile electrons)
- Relatively high melting points (Fe: 1538°C); lower than ionic/covalent bonds of similar coordination

### Van der Waals (Secondary) Bonding
Weak electrostatic interactions between atoms or molecules with temporary or permanent dipoles.

Examples: graphite (between layers), polymers (between chain segments)

Properties:
- Weak → low melting points, low stiffness for polymers
- Important in amorphous polymers where entangled chains interact via secondary bonds

## Material Classes and Bonding

| Class | Primary Bonding | Typical Properties |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Metals** | Metallic | Ductile, conductive, moderate-high stiffness |
| **Ceramics** | Ionic/Covalent | Brittle, hard, high melting point, insulating |
| **Polymers** | Covalent + Van der Waals | Flexible or rigid, low density, poor heat resistance |
| **Composites** | Mixed | Tailored by combining matrix and reinforcement |

---

## Material Properties Classification

Key mechanical properties (from tensile testing):
- **Young's modulus E**: stiffness (slope of elastic stress-strain curve); bonding type governs E
- **Yield strength σ_y**: stress at which permanent plastic deformation begins
- **UTS**: ultimate tensile strength (peak stress on engineering stress-strain curve)
- **Ductility**: elongation to failure — metals (ductile) vs ceramics (brittle)
- **Toughness**: area under the stress-strain curve = energy absorbed before fracture

These properties are modified by [[crystal-structures|microstructure]], [[heat-treatment|heat treatment]], and processing.
